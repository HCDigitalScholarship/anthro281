Land use has long been a point of conflict between the Gwich’in people, oil drilling corporations, and the U.S. and Canadian Governments. The Gwich’in people can be found in 15 communities across Northeast Alaska and Canada, specifically in British Columbia and Yukon. The Porcupine Caribou herd spans a similar area, overlapping with the Arctic National Wildlife Refuge. Still, just a few miles away from these sacred animals are over 100 oil drilling sites. The pollution from those sites creates an immense health risk not only for the Caribou and other animals within the Arctic National Refuge, but for the Gwich’in people themselves. Additionally, this is yet another example of the appropriation of Gwich’in sacred land. This has further increased tension around how land should be used, and who has the right to use it. Mapping caribou migration, oil drilling sites, land use, and Gwich’in language and communities aids in creating a broader understanding of this complicated issue and its history. Below, we compiled a few maps and sources that highlight how land is used and creates tension.
